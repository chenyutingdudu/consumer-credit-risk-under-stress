# Research narrative (plain language)

## One-sentence summary

This work studies how machine learning models used in credit decisions relate to financial risk and uneven borrower outcomes when conditions resemble economic stress.

## Extended summary

The gap between predictive accuracy and real-world decision quality motivates the analysis. In consumer credit, a model affects lending decisions, borrower access, and institutional risk. Using public credit default data, the project evaluates how model errors translate into financial loss and how those errors change under a simulated stress scenario. Methods combine statistical learning, machine learning, subgroup analysis, stress testing, and decision-aware evaluation.

## Short technical introduction

The analysis examines predictive bias and decision risk in consumer credit models under economic stress. Beyond accuracy alone, prediction errors are mapped to false approvals, false rejections, expected financial loss, and subgroup-level risk disparities.

## Core technical takeaway

Two models can have similar accuracy but very different financial consequences. That motivates decision-focused evaluation and statistically grounded reporting beyond aggregate classification metrics.
